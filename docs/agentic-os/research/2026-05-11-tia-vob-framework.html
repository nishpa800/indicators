2. The thesis — why three properties, not one

Anish's observation: certain VOB lines are impervious. Price tests them and gets rejected. They have a quality you can see on the chart — "the candles coming in wouldn't even dare. They've felt how antifragile and how impervious and how tensile that level is." The challenge is to codify "you can see it" into an equation that fires at the earliest moment we can know with stated confidence.

A naive approach would compress everything into a single "strength" scalar — pool volume, perhaps. That fails because different lines fail in different ways:

  • Thin lines get sliced through immediately (low imperviousness). You can see them as single touches that go straight through.
  • Brittle lines look strong (high pool volume) until they break catastrophically (low tensile). One major attempt punches through; the level shatters rather than flexing.
  • Fragile lines erode under repeated tests (anti-antifragile). The 5th touch is weaker than the 1st. Smart money knows this and grinds them.

The thesis: a line that holds has all three qualities. It is hard to penetrate (imperviousness), it absorbs stress without breaking (tensile), and it strengthens under repeated tests (antifragility). Conversely, a line that blows through lacks at least one. The TIA framework decomposes the single intuition into three measurable dimensions and recombines them into a probability.

Figure 1 — The TIA triangle. Three independent material properties combine into the master holding probability P(t).

3. Imperviousness — materials science of hardness

IMPERVIOUSNESS = the property of resisting penetration. In materials science, imperviousness has two related but distinct scalar measures: hardness (resistance to indentation) and ballistic resistance (resistance to high-velocity penetration). Both inform the TIA imperviousness equation.

3.2 First principles of hardness

The Gao-Tian equation for theoretical hardness of covalent crystals:

HV = 350 · Ne2/3 · e−1.191 fi · d−2.5

where Ne is the electron density of the strongest bond, fi is the ionicity of the bond, and d is the bond length in Å. The dominant terms: bond density and bond length (shorter is harder).

The simpler scaling argument: hardness ∝ bond energy × atomic packing density ÷ atomic radius. Diamond wins because all three terms maximize simultaneously.

3.3 Ballistic resistance — different from hardness

Ballistic resistance follows the Florence equation for projectile defeat:

V50 ∝ √( σy · t / ρp )

where V50 is the velocity at which 50% of projectiles defeat the target. σy is target yield strength, t is thickness, ρp is projectile density. Thickness and yield strength matter LINEARLY but together; you can have high hardness with low ballistic resistance (brittle materials shatter under impact even if they're hard against slow indenters).

3.4 The VOB imperviousness analogy

I(t) measures how hard it would be for price to penetrate the level — combining the "bond density" of the level (pool volume) with its "packing density" (how many tiers / zones are clustered there) and the "atomic radius" (distance from current price).

An impervious VOB level has:

  • High accumulated volume in the zone (analogue of high bond density)
  • Multiple sensitivity tiers agreeing at the same price level (analogue of high atomic packing density — multiple "atoms" of confluence)
  • Cross-indicator confluence — HV+FVG+GZ1 (Sat 1) or other satellites also mark this price (analogue of crystalline regularity)
  • Tight relative spacing (the zone is THIN — small upper-to-lower range — like a dense crystal vs a loose lattice)

4. Tensile — toughness as area under the stress-strain curve

TENSILE = the property of absorbing stress without permanent deformation, then returning to original shape. The naive measure is ultimate tensile strength (UTS, the maximum stress before failure). The correct measure for our purpose is toughness: the integrated area under the stress-strain curve. A high-UTS / low-elongation material is brittle (fails catastrophically). A moderate-UTS / high-elongation material is tough (absorbs lots of energy before failing).

The key insight from spider silk. Darwin's bark spider produces the toughest material known in nature. Not the strongest (graphene wins UTS), not the most elastic (rubber wins strain), but the toughest. Why? Because toughness = strength × elongation, and spider silk optimizes for the integral, not for either endpoint. The hierarchical structure (nanocrystalline β-sheets in an amorphous matrix) means small stresses are absorbed elastically by the matrix; large stresses crystallize the matrix, recruiting more β-sheets — a self-amplifying response. This is the right analogue for a VOB level: small price probes are absorbed elastically; large probes recruit more accumulated volume into defense.

4.2 The stress-strain curve and the toughness integral

Figure 2 — Stress-strain curves. Toughness is the AREA under the curve. Brittle materials have high peak stress but tiny area (small useful energy absorbed). Tough materials have moderate peak stress and large area.

4.3 The VOB tensile analogy

T(t) measures how much "energy" a level can absorb in retests without failing. The metric is the area under the analogue of the stress-strain curve: pool volume (UTS analogue) × elongation capacity (how deep price can probe and still get rejected).

A tensile VOB level has:

  • Sufficient pool volume to mount a defense (UTS analogue — there must be SOME strength to begin with)
  • Historical evidence that price has entered the zone (elongated under stress) AND been rejected back out (no permanent deformation) — high success rate at probes
  • Multiple successful retests across different volume profiles (the level has been tested elastically and re-shaped without breaking)
  • The maximum penetration depth on prior retests stays below ~50% of the zone width (the level deformed but didn't yield)

5. Antifragility — Taleb's formalism plus materials examples

ANTIFRAGILITY = the property of GAINING strength from stress, volatility, and randomness. Coined by Nassim Taleb in Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder (2012). Antifragility is not robustness (which merely resists harm) — it is the inverse of fragility. Fragile things hate volatility; antifragile things require it.

5.1 Taleb's formal definition

A system's response to a stressor X is described by a payoff function W(X). The system is:

  • Fragile if W''(X) < 0 at relevant scales (concave payoff; Jensen's inequality says variance hurts)
  • Robust if W''(X) ≈ 0 (linear payoff; insensitive to variance)
  • Antifragile if W''(X) > 0 (convex payoff; variance helps)

Jensen's inequality formalizes the variance benefit:

For convex f:    E[f(X)] ≥ f(E[X])

That is: if your payoff function is convex (antifragile), MORE volatility in the input → BETTER expected output. This is the mathematical heart of antifragility.

5.3 Mithridatization — the original antifragile protocol

Mithridates VI of Pontus (134–63 BCE) is reported to have ingested sub-lethal doses of poison daily to build immunity. The protocol — graduated exposure to a stressor — is the prototype of all hormesis-class antifragility. When Mithridates attempted suicide by poison after defeat, he failed and had to ask a soldier to kill him by sword. The system had become so antifragile to its stressor that the stressor no longer worked at all.

5.4 The convexity plot

Figure 3 — Convexity classifies systems. Antifragile (convex up): payoff accelerates with stress. Fragile (concave down): payoff collapses under stress. The second derivative is the discriminator.

5.5 The VOB antifragility analogy

A(t) measures whether the level GETS STRONGER after being tested. The convexity of pool volume (or success rate) with respect to test count. Each successful rejection recruits more volume into defense.

An antifragile VOB level has:

  • Pool volume that INCREASES across retest events (work hardening — defenders pile in when probed)
  • Touches that came on high volume AND held (Hadfield steel — the harder you hit it, the harder it gets)
  • Aged levels with repeated successful tests (Lindy effect — the longer a non-perishable info survives, the longer it will keep surviving)
  • Conditional on volume regime: tests during high-volume sessions held more often than tests during low-volume sessions (positive correlation between stressor magnitude and survival)
  • The Wolff's-law analogue: zones at price levels with the most "stress" (most volume churn nearby) have the most accumulated volume — strength concentrates where stress is highest

7. First-principles equations for I, T, A, and master P

This section derives the three property equations from first principles and combines them into the master probability. Every term has a physical interpretation and a concrete metadata mapping (§8).

7.1 Imperviousness I(t) — derivation

Starting from the Gao-Tian hardness equation HV ∝ Ne2/3 · d−2.5, we identify the analogues:

  • Ne (electron density of strongest bond) → pool volume of the zone × cross-tier agreement count
  • d (bond length) → distance from current price to zone midpoint, as fraction

The raw imperviousness signal:

Iraw(t) = (vzone(t) · ntier(t)) · nstack(t)1/2 ÷ (1 + dprice(t))5/2

where:

  • vzone(t) = volume of THIS zone, normalized to its tier's median volume (so v=1 means median strength)
  • ntier(t) ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} = number of sensitivity tiers (A–F) with an active zone at this price level (cross-tier confluence)
  • nstack(t) ∈ {0, 1, 2, ...} = number of zones (across all tiers, all directions) within stack_thresh_pct (default 2%) of current price
  • dprice(t) = |close(t) − zone_mid(t)| ÷ close(t), as fraction

Normalize to [0, 1] via logistic transform with empirical scale parameters:

I(t) = 1 / (1 + e−αI(Iraw(t) − βI))

where αI and βI are calibrated against historical hold/penetrate outcomes. Initial values: αI = 0.5, βI = median(Iraw) over training sample.

7.2 Tensile T(t) — derivation

Toughness K = ∫σ dε. Discretize the stress-strain curve as a series of touch events with associated stress (touch volume) and strain (penetration depth):

KVOB(t) = Σi=1..k [ vtouch,i · εtouch,i · 𝟙{rejectedi} ]

where k = total touch count, εtouch,i = penetration depth on touch i (as fraction of zone width, capped at 1.0), 𝟙{rejectedi} is 1 if touch was rejected (price exited zone in same direction it entered) else 0.

Combine with current pool volume (the UTS analogue) and the success rate (the elastic-recovery analogue):

Traw(t) = vpoolnormalized(t) · rsuccess(t) · (1 + KVOB(t))

where rsuccess(t) = successful_rejections ÷ max(total_touches, 1).

For new zones with no touches: KVOB = 0, rsuccess = bootstrapped to the tier's median rsuccess over the past 90 days (Bayesian prior). This gives T(t) immediate value even at formation.

Normalize:

T(t) = 1 / (1 + e−αT(Traw(t) − βT))

7.3 Antifragility A(t) — derivation

From Taleb's formalism, A is the SECOND derivative of strength with respect to stress. In practice, fit a quadratic to the touch-event sequence and take the curvature coefficient. Let strength_proxy(i) = cumulative pool volume after touch i. Fit S(i) = a + b·i + c·i² and extract c. The antifragility signal is the sign and magnitude of c.

c(t) = argminc Σi=1..k (S(i) − (a + b·i + c·i²))²

Equivalent simpler operator (more robust for small k): the second-difference of cumulative strength.

Araw(t) = (1 / k) · Σi=3..k [ S(i) − 2·S(i−1) + S(i−2) ]

Augment with a Lindy term for level age:

Alindy(t) = (k + 1) / (age_bars(t) / median_age + 1)

and a Hadfield term capturing whether high-volume tests held more often than low-volume tests:

Ahadfield(t) = E[vtouch | rejected] / E[vtouch | penetrated]

(With smoothing constants for zero-division on new zones: add 1 to numerator and denominator counts.)

Combined antifragility signal:

Araw, comb(t) = Araw(t) · Alindy(t)γL · Ahadfield(t)γH

with default exponents γL = 0.5 and γH = 1.0 (Hadfield-weighted; Lindy is a moderate multiplier). Normalize:

A(t) = 1 / (1 + e−αA(Araw, comb(t) − βA))

7.4 Master probability P(t)

Three options for combining I, T, A into the master probability. We propose weighted geometric mean for the initial release, with equal weights, then re-calibrate weights via logistic regression once we have outcome data:

P(t) = (I(t)wI · T(t)wT · A(t)wA)1 / (wI + wT + wA)

with initial weights wI = wT = wA = 1 (equal). The geometric mean has the critical property that if any one of I, T, A is near zero, P is near zero — the level fails even if the other two dimensions are strong. This matches Anish's "all three qualities" requirement.

Why geometric mean and not multiplicative (P = I × T × A)? Both are zero-dominated, but the geometric mean stays in [0, 1] naturally without renormalization. Multiplicative would produce very small numbers (0.5³ = 0.125) that confuse the threshold interpretation.

Future work: replace with a calibrated logistic regression once we have 30+ days of outcome data:

Pv2(t) = 1 / (1 + e−(β₀ + βI·I(t) + βT·T(t) + βA·A(t) + interaction terms))

9. The three thresholds — why 50% / 62.5% / 87.5%

Anish's specification: three detection plots per direction at 50%, ~62%, ~87% probabilities. The question is whether these specific numbers are calibration-optimal or merely intuition-driven. Both perspectives below.

9.1 The intuition case

ThresholdNumerical rationaleMental model
50% (1/2)Coin flip baseline. Below 50% the level offers no information — could go either way. ABOVE 50% the level holds more often than not."Worth watching." Earliest actionable signal. Maximum recall, modest precision.
62.5% (5/8)Fibonacci-adjacent: 0.618 is the inverse golden ratio, 5/8 = 0.625 ≈ φ⁻¹. Canonical in trader vocabulary (Fibonacci retracements)."Confirmation." 5:3 odds the level holds. Reasonable conviction without overreach.
87.5% (7/8)Strong asymmetric conviction. 7:1 odds. Stops short of 95% (which is overconfident given model misspecification risk)."Ironclad." High precision; trade with size.

9.2 The calibration case

After 90 days of outcomes accumulated through the TIA system, we can produce a calibration curve: for each predicted probability bucket, what fraction actually held? A perfectly calibrated system has predicted-P equal to realized-rate across all buckets.

If realized rates deviate, we apply isotonic regression to the calibration curve to find adjusted thresholds where the realized rate matches the stated rate. Initial settings stay at 0.50/0.625/0.875 (intuition); after 90 days, re-fit.

9.3 Why three thresholds, not one or five

One threshold loses the gradient information; we'd treat 51% the same as 95% — wasting the model's discrimination power. Five thresholds (e.g., 50/60/70/80/90) fragment the alert stream into bands that traders can't reliably distinguish — operator noise overwhelms signal.

Three is the minimum that preserves a meaningful gradient (low/medium/high conviction) without overwhelming the operator. Aligned with the OS-wide three-tier structure (grunt / knowledge / owner).

10. Latency analysis — earliest-moment detection bound

Anish's hard requirement: "Immediately when it's fifty percent... emit that detection plot and alert it. The fastest you can possibly know with that certainty level." This section bounds the latency from condition-crossing to alert emission.

10.2 Decision latency

The decision to emit the detection plot is bounded by the time from "bar closes" to "alert fires." Pine evaluates indicators on bar close; alertcondition + alert are evaluated within the same evaluation pass. So latency is < 100ms from bar close to alert dispatch in normal conditions.

To go FASTER than bar-close requires real-time tick-level evaluation with barstate.isconfirmed = false, which introduces repaint risk (a level that "fires" at 50% mid-bar may drop below 50% by bar close). Recommendation: stay at bar-close evaluation for D₂ and D₃ (high conviction); experiment with real-time eval ONLY for D₁ (50% threshold, exploratory). If real-time D₁ generates too many flips, demote to bar-close.

10.3 Cold-start latency

A new zone has zero touches and zero outcomes. The TIA score is computable from formation thanks to the Bayesian bootstrap (§7.2, §7.3) — we use tier-median rsuccess, Hadfield ratio, and Lindy term to initialize. So D₁ can fire on the SAME bar a new zone forms if I(t) is already extreme enough. T(t) and A(t) lag — but they're bootstrapped at neutral so they don't actively SUPPRESS detection.

A level with I=0.95 + T=0.30 + A=0.30 was treated as weaker than I=0.65 + T=0.65 + A=0.65, but in reality the first case was a stronger hold."HIGHTest the three combinations (geometric, logistic-regression, max-of-three) against held-out validation data. Pick the highest AUROC.
3"Thresholds 0.50/0.625/0.875 were pulled from intuition. They didn't align with realized calibration. D₁ fires happened in 35% of cases, not 50%, so traders lost trust."HIGHEmpirical isotonic regression on the first 90 days. Adjust thresholds so realized rates match stated rates.
4"Antifragility was conflated with selection bias. We only observed levels that survived prior tests; the 'antifragile' ones were merely the survivors. The A score was a tautology."MEDIUMTrack failed levels (formed-then-blown-through) explicitly. Compute A on both survivors and failures. Validate A discriminates.
5"The Lindy term inverted for HFT-discovered levels. Older levels were MORE likely to get blown through because everyone knew them and HFT picked them off in liquidity sweeps."MEDIUMInclude a 'discovery timestamp' variable. Partition Lindy: aged-AND-recently-tested = strong; aged-AND-untested = weak.
6"Latency was too high. By the time enough touch data accumulated for A(t) to converge, the move was already gone."MEDIUMBayesian bootstrap of A at zone formation (§7.3). Don't require touches before firing D₁ — let I dominate early, with A's neutral prior staying out of the way.
7"Pool volume on bull/bear side was gamed by spoofing. Large-volume zones formed at price levels where there was no real interest, just liquidity-pull tactics."LOW-MEDIUMCompare pool volume to time-of-day baselines. Flag anomalies. Optionally: weight zone volume by depth-of-book stability (would require new data).
8"The system overfit the training regime. Worked in trending markets where retests are infrequent and clean. Failed in chop where every bar tests every level repeatedly."HIGHRegime-conditional calibration via the axis-decomposition skill (SD-013). Maintain separate threshold sets for trend / range / gap-up / gap-down / inside-day regimes.

12. Inversion — the fragility equation for blow-through lines

Per the inversion skill (SD-013 + Munger), instead of asking "what makes a level hold?" ask "what guarantees a level blows through?" The inverse equation gives us a SECOND useful signal: detection of fragile levels we should EXPECT to fail.

12.1 Fragility classes

ClassDefinitionDetectionTrading implication
ThinLow I — small pool, no tier confluence, no stackI(t) < 0.20Don't trust this level. Trade THROUGH it.
BrittleHigh I + low T — looks strong but has never been tested or all tests failedI(t) > 0.70 AND rsuccess < 0.30 AND k ≥ 3Probable trap — the pool is big but the level lacks elasticity. First major test will shatter it.
ErodingAntifragile gone negative — touches coming on declining volume and shallower rejectionsA(t) decreasing trend over last 3 touches; c < 0 in quadratic fit"Former monolith showing cracks." Don't add to longs (bull zone) or shorts (bear zone) — exit on next test.
Lindy-staleAged level with no recent testsage_bars > 500 AND last_touch_age > 200Treat as unknown. The system has no evidence it still holds.

12.2 The fragility equation

Mirror the P equation:

F(t) = 1 − P(t) = 1 − (I(t) · T(t) · A(t))1/3

plus class-specific overrides (the four classes above produce a class label, not just a scalar). Three blow-through detection plots:

  • F₁(t): F(t) ≥ 0.50 — earliest warning
  • F₂(t): F(t) ≥ 0.625 — confirmed weakness
  • F₃(t): F(t) ≥ 0.875 — imminent break

And one additional cross-class detection plot: "Former Monolith" = F(t) crossed up from below 0.30 (a level that was previously strong but is now showing weakness).

13. Validation protocol — how we test before going live

  1. Variable correlation audit (Phase V1). For each metadata variable (pool volume, stack count, tier agreement, gap, slope, success rate, touch count, age, Hadfield ratio), compute its individual correlation with the binary outcome (level held vs blew through) over a 90-day historical sample. Drop variables with correlation < 0.05 in absolute value. Goal: ensure every term in the equation earns its keep.
  2. Combination test (Phase V2). Compute I, T, A for every historical level. Test three combination strategies: geometric mean, multiplicative, max-of-three, and weighted logistic regression. Compare AUROC on a held-out 30-day validation window. Pick the winner.
  3. Threshold calibration (Phase V3). For each candidate P combination, plot the reliability curve (predicted P vs realized hold rate). Apply isotonic regression. Find the predicted-P that corresponds to actual-P = 0.50, 0.625, 0.875. These become the empirical thresholds.
  4. Regime partitioning (Phase V4). Re-run V3 separately for each regime axis value (trend, range, gap-up, gap-down, inside-day). Verify the thresholds are stable across regimes; if not, ship regime-conditional thresholds.
  5. Latency profiling (Phase V5). Measure end-to-end latency from bar close to alert fire on a sample of TradingView accounts. Confirm < 1 second.
  6. Inversion validation (Phase V6). Repeat V1–V3 for the fragility equation. Verify F₃ events predict blow-through within 5 bars at > 70% rate (the empirical "fragility hit rate").
  7. A/B canary (Phase V7). Run TIA in shadow alongside the existing canonical VOB for 14 days. Compare: number of alerts, alert quality, false positive rate, missed events. If TIA wins on all four metrics, promote to canonical.

17. Glossary

TermMeaning
TIATensile-Impervious-Antifragile. The three-property framework defined in this doc. Replaces individual T3 and zone-formation detection plots in vob-asym canonical with six probability-tiered detection plots (3 bull + 3 bear).
Imperviousness I(t)Property of resisting penetration. Scalar in [0, 1]. Computed from pool volume, tier-confluence count, stack count, and distance to current price.
Tensile T(t)Property of flexing under stress and returning to shape. Scalar in [0, 1]. Computed from pool volume, success rate of past touches, and the toughness integral over touch history.
Antifragility A(t)Property of gaining strength from stress. Scalar in [0, 1]. Computed from the convexity of strength vs touch count (Taleb), Hadfield ratio (high-volume tests vs low-volume tests), and Lindy term (age × test frequency).
Master probability P(t)The composite hold-probability. P(t) = (I · T · A)1/3 (geometric mean). Equal weights initially; logistic regression after 90 days of outcomes.
Fragility F(t)1 − P(t). The complement: probability the level blows through.
Detection plots D₁, D₂, D₃The three hold-direction detection plots at thresholds 0.50, 0.625, 0.875 of P. Fire on first crossing from below.
Detection plots F₁, F₂, F₃The three blow-through detection plots at thresholds 0.50, 0.625, 0.875 of F. Mirror structure.
Touch eventBar at which price enters a previously-untouched zone (or re-enters after exiting). Tracked per-zone in the new state machine.
RejectionOutcome of a touch where price exited the zone in the same direction it entered, within the outcome_window (default 5 bars).
PenetrationOutcome of a touch where price exited the zone in the OPPOSITE direction (crossed all the way through). The "failure" outcome.
Hadfield ratioAntifragility term: E[touch_volume | rejected] / E[touch_volume | penetrated]. > 1 means high-volume tests held (antifragile); < 1 means high-volume tests broke through (fragile).
Lindy termAntifragility term: (touch_count + 1) / (age_bars / median_age + 1). Captures the "what has survived will keep surviving" effect, normalized.
Toughness KΣ (touch_volume × penetration_depth × 𝟙{rejected}) over the zone's touch history. The discrete analogue of the stress-strain area integral.
Pancake / BraidedAnish's vocabulary for the visual appearance of multiple sensitivity tiers' zones at the same price level. Maps directly to the n_tier and n_stack variables in I(t).
Singular lineAn individual T3 buy/sell or zone-formation marker, of which there are 13 + 12 = 25 in the current canonical vob-asym. TIA replaces these with six probability-tiered detection plots in the new TIA file.
