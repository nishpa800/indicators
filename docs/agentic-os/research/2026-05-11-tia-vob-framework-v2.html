3. The thesis — recap from v1

A VOB level holds when it has three independent properties: imperviousness (resistance to penetration), tensile (absorbs stress and returns to shape), antifragility (gains strength from stress). A naive single-scalar "strength" model fails because different levels fail in different ways — thin levels get sliced (low I), brittle levels shatter (low T), eroding levels weaken under repeated tests (low A). The TIA framework decomposes the single intuition into three measurable dimensions and recombines them into a master probability P(t) = (I·T·A)^(1/3). Three detection plots fire when P crosses 0.50, 0.625, 0.875 — three bull, three bear. Inversion gives a parallel fragility F(t) = 1 − P(t) with three blow-through detection plots at the same thresholds.

4. Imperviousness deep dive — defense, chemicals, environmental

IMPERVIOUSNESS = resistance to penetration by an external probe. Three sub-fields of materials and defense science measure this directly. We extract first-principles equations from each and map every term to a measurable variable we can compute from TradingView data + our indicator library.

4.1 Defense ballistics — penetration mechanics

Modern armor design rests on a small set of canonical equations. Each describes the limit at which a projectile of given mass, velocity, and geometry defeats a target of given density, strength, and thickness. We map projectile → market-side incoming volume probe, target → VOB level defense.

4.1.1 The Tate-Alekseevskii long-rod penetration equation

For a long-rod projectile (length ≫ diameter) penetrating a semi-infinite target at hydrodynamic velocity:

½ ρp (v − u)² + Yp = ½ ρt u² + Rt

where v = rod velocity, u = penetration velocity, ρp, ρt = densities of projectile and target, Yp = dynamic flow stress of projectile, Rt = target resistance to penetration (Tate's Rt, typically 4–6× the target's static yield strength). Penetration depth P_depth at exhaustion:

Pdepth = L0 × √(ρp/ρt) × [v / (v + √(2(Rt−Yp)/ρp))]

Market mapping. The incoming volume probe (price moving into the zone) is the projectile; the accumulated pool volume + cross-tier confluence is the target's effective Rt. The penetration depth analogue is how far price travels INTO the zone before being repulsed. We compute (Rt)VOB := pool_volume_normalized × tier_agreement × stack_count, which goes into I-VAR-1 Rt.

4.1.2 Walker-Anderson ceramic armor model

For ceramic-faced composite armor (typical in modern body armor), Walker and Anderson (1995, 2003) refined Tate to handle the ceramic conoid dwell phase:

V502 = (2 × ρt × σt × h × cos²θ) / (ρp × d²)

where V50 is the 50% defeat velocity, σt the target strength, h the target thickness, θ the obliquity angle, d the projectile diameter. Mapping. Obliquity → angle of attack of incoming price (slope of fast EMA at moment of probe). Thickness → zone width (upper-lower spread). The TIA imperviousness equation inherits this thickness-times-strength scaling.

4.1.3 Lambert-Jonas residual velocity for finite-thickness plates

The empirical Lambert-Jonas equation predicts residual velocity after defeating a plate:

Vrp = (Vsp − VBLp)

where VBL is the ballistic limit, Vs the strike velocity, and p ≈ 2.0 for ductile targets. Mapping. If price exceeds the "ballistic limit" of the zone, it continues with reduced velocity — the residual move is dampened by the defended level. The exponent p ≈ 2 means kinetic energy is the conserved quantity, not momentum. TIA's tensile T(t) captures the residual-velocity damping (zones that DON'T fully halt price but slow it have positive T even if low I).

4.1.4 Florence ballistic limit equation

For multi-ply textile armor (Kevlar, Dyneema), Florence (1969):

VBL = √(2 × εf × El × A / m)

where εf = strain to failure, El = longitudinal modulus, A = areal density, m = projectile mass. Critical insight: εf (strain to failure) is a TENSILE property — meaning ballistic resistance depends on BOTH stiffness AND elongation. This is why we can't compute imperviousness in isolation from tensile.

4.1.5 Recht-Ipson momentum-balance penetration

Vr = Mp / (Mp + Mplug) × √(Vs2 − VBL2)

where Mplug is the mass of material ejected from the target on penetration. Mapping. A penetration event in a VOB zone "ejects" some pool volume (defenders that gave up). The Recht-Ipson plug mass corresponds to how much defensive volume is consumed per failed defense event. Variable: I-VAR-7 plug_consumption = (pool_before_failed_defense − pool_after) / pool_before.

4.1.6 Krieg-Swift-Pinaire ballistic limit (simplified engineering form)

VBL = K × √(σy × t / ρp)

Compact engineering version. Demonstrates that ballistic limit scales as square root of (yield strength × thickness). Mapping. Doubling pool volume only increases imperviousness by √2, not 2×. This is encoded in the I(t) equation's square-root term on the stack count.

4.2 Materials hardness — indentation resistance

Defense ballistics measures impact/dynamic penetration. The static analogue is hardness — resistance to a slow indenter. Useful because "static" tests of VOB levels (low-volume probes that slowly chip away) are also resisted by the same fundamental imperviousness.

4.3 Chemicals — barrier permeability

Imperviousness in the chemical world is barrier performance: oxygen, water vapor, CO₂ transmission through polymer films. The standard formula:

P = D × S

where P is the permeability coefficient, D the diffusion coefficient (m²/s), S the solubility coefficient (mol/(m³·Pa)). Multi-layer barriers:

1/Ptotal = Σi (Li / (Pi × Ltotal))

4.3.1 The Salame permachor model

Permachor π is an empirical parameter aggregating polymer cohesive energy and free volume:

ln(P) = ln(P0) − s × π

where P0 is a reference permeability and s is a substance-specific scaling. Mapping. A VOB level's "permachor" is the aggregated cohesion across its supporting indicators. High HVD+PBJ confluence + HV+FVG+GZ1 marking + Pentagon firing = high permachor = low permeability = high imperviousness.

4.4 Environmental — radiation and EMI shielding

4.4.1 Beer-Lambert law for radiation attenuation

I(x) = I0 × e−μ x

where μ is the linear attenuation coefficient (m⁻¹) and x is the thickness traversed. The half-value layer (HVL) — thickness reducing intensity by half — is:

HVL = ln(2) / μ

Mass attenuation coefficient μ/ρ is the density-normalized version, useful for cross-material comparison. Mapping. The volume-attenuation coefficient of a VOB level := total pool volume / zone width. The "half-value layer" of the level := how much price movement consumes half the defending volume. Variable: I-VAR-12 HVL_volume.

4.4.2 Schelkunoff EMI shielding effectiveness

SEdB = A + R + B

where A = absorption loss, R = reflection loss, B = multiple-reflection correction. Each component is a function of frequency, conductivity, permeability, and thickness. Mapping. A VOB level resists "absorption" (price probes into the zone that get dampened — tensile) AND "reflection" (price bounces off without entering — pure imperviousness). The combined SE_dB analogue is what we ultimately want.

5. Tensile deep dive — toughness, fracture, hierarchical materials

TENSILE = capacity to absorb stress without permanent damage, then return to shape. The naive metric is UTS (peak stress before failure). The CORRECT metric is toughness — the integrated area under the stress-strain curve. Strength alone is brittleness; strength × elasticity is toughness.

5.1 The toughness integral

Ktoughness = ∫0εf σ(ε) dε

For a power-law plastic material (Hollomon): σ = K × εn, the toughness becomes:

Ktoughness = K × εfn+1 / (n+1)

Mapping. Each touch event of a VOB zone produces a (stress, strain) data point: stress = touch volume, strain = penetration depth into zone. Sum across touches with rejection-flag weighting gives the discrete toughness analogue. Variable: T-VAR-3 K_discrete.

5.2 Fracture mechanics — when toughness fails

5.2.1 Griffith's criterion (1921)

σc = √(2 E γs / (π a))

where σc = critical stress for crack propagation, E = Young's modulus, γs = surface energy per unit area, a = half-crack length. Mapping. A VOB zone has a "critical incoming volume" above which a single probe shatters defense. Below σ_c, probes are absorbed; above, the zone fractures. The relevant variable is the zone's structural cohesion: T-VAR-4 sigma_c.

5.2.2 Irwin's stress intensity factor

KI = σ × √(π a) × Y

where Y is a dimensionless geometric factor. Fracture toughness KIC is the critical KI at which crack propagation becomes unstable. Mapping. Each touch event creates a "crack" of size proportional to penetration depth; if K_I exceeds K_IC, the zone fails catastrophically.

5.2.3 J-integral (Rice 1968) — for nonlinear elastic-plastic materials

J = ∫Γ (W dy − Ti × ∂ui/∂x ds)

The J-integral is path-independent and equals the energy release rate. For VOB: J is the total energy released per unit "crack advance" (per unit zone penetration). High J = brittle (energy released quickly = catastrophic failure). Low J = tough (energy absorbed gradually).

5.2.4 Paris law (1961) — fatigue crack growth

da/dN = C × (ΔK)m

where da/dN = crack growth per stress cycle, ΔK = stress intensity range, m typically 2–4. Mapping. Each touch event is one fatigue cycle. The Paris exponent m governs how quickly accumulated micro-damage compounds. Variable: T-VAR-6 paris_m_estimate — fitted from the zone's touch history.

5.2.5 Coffin-Manson low-cycle fatigue (1954)

Δεp/2 = εf' × (2Nf)c

For low-cycle, high-strain fatigue (the regime where a VOB zone takes a few high-energy hits rather than many small ones). The exponent c ≈ −0.5 for most materials. Combined with Basquin's high-cycle equation:

Δε/2 = (σf'/E) × (2Nf)b + εf' × (2Nf)c

Captures both elastic and plastic strain regimes — the full fatigue life. Mapping. A VOB zone has a finite fatigue life. Predict it from touch history: T-VAR-7 N_f_remaining.

5.3 Modulus of resilience and modulus of toughness

Ur = σy2 / (2 E) (resilience — elastic strain energy density)
Ut ≈ ½ × (σy + σUTS) × εf (toughness approximation)

Resilience is the energy stored in elastic deformation that returns to the system on release. Toughness includes plastic deformation that doesn't return. Both contribute to tensile T(t).

5.4 Hierarchical materials — nature's toughness optimization

5.4.1 Spider silk (Caerostris darwini, Darwin's bark spider)

Toughness ≈ 520 MJ/m³ — the highest known in nature. Hierarchical structure:

  • Level 1 (Å scale): H-bonded β-sheet nanocrystals; high stiffness
  • Level 2 (nm scale): Amorphous matrix surrounding crystallites; absorbs energy elastically
  • Level 3 (μm scale): Hierarchical bundling; redirects force across fibrils

The Keten-Buehler (2010) model gives the strength-elongation product:

σsilk × εsilk ≈ σβ-sheet × fc × εamorphous × (1 − fc)

where f_c is the crystalline volume fraction. Optimal f_c ≈ 0.4 maximizes toughness. Mapping. A VOB level's "hierarchical structure" is the layering of (a) pool volume — Level 1 strength, (b) cross-tier confluence — Level 2 matrix, (c) cross-indicator confluence — Level 3 redirection. Variable: T-VAR-10 hierarchy_score.

5.4.2 Nacre (mother-of-pearl) — the brick-and-mortar model

Aragonite tablets (95% by volume) bonded by biopolymer mortar (5%). Toughness ~3000× single-crystal aragonite. Wegst-Ashby (2004) brick-and-mortar:

σcomposite ∝ σbrick × √(τmortar × tbrick / Lbrick)

Mapping: bricks = high-volume zones, mortar = the cross-tier MutEx overlap engine binding them. The √(thickness × shear) scaling reappears in TIA.

5.4.3 Bone — hierarchical biocomposite (Wegst, Ashby, Currey)

Cortical bone toughness scales with mineral fraction f_m by a power law:

KIC(bone) = K0 × (1 − fm)n

with n ≈ 1.5. Counterintuitive: too much mineral makes bone brittle; the collagen matrix's elasticity contributes critical toughness. Mapping. Too much pool volume CONCENTRATED in one tier (no diversification across sensitivities) makes the level brittle. Diversification across tiers = collagen matrix = critical for tensile.

5.5 Elastomers — when nonlinearity matters

5.5.1 Mooney-Rivlin (large strain elastomers)

σ = 2 (λ − λ−2) × (C1 + C2/λ)

For VOB levels that "stretch" significantly under load — large penetration events that get rejected. The Mooney-Rivlin behavior captures the stiffening response at large strain (rubber bands snap back HARDER from full stretch than from half stretch — same mechanism by which a deeply-probed VOB level may bounce HARDER than a lightly-probed one).

6. Antifragility deep dive — Taleb-Douady, work hardening, Wolff's law, hormesis

ANTIFRAGILITY = property of GAINING from stress, volatility, or randomness. Coined by Taleb. Formal definition is positive second derivative of payoff w.r.t. stressor. Real-world materials examples are quantitatively documented in metallurgy, biology, and toxicology.

6.1 Taleb-Douady (2013) — the antifragility detection operator

Taleb and Douady give a rigorous detector for antifragility absent a closed-form payoff function. For a system with state X subject to a perturbation of magnitude K:

H(X, f, K, s, n) = (f(X+K) + f(X−K)) / 2 − f(X)
  • H > 0 → antifragile (mean of perturbed payoffs exceeds unperturbed = convex)
  • H ≈ 0 → robust (linear payoff)
  • H < 0 → fragile (concave payoff; variance hurts)

This is the discrete second-difference test for convexity. Mapping. For a VOB level, we evaluate H by simulating "perturbed" volume probes ±K standard deviations from the mean probe size and comparing held-rate to the unperturbed mean probe. Empirical implementation: bucket touch events into low-volume / medium-volume / high-volume groups; compute hold rates; H = avg(low_hold_rate, high_hold_rate) − medium_hold_rate. Variable: A-VAR-1 H_taleb.

6.2 Jensen's inequality — the variance benefit

For convex f: E[f(X)] ≥ f(E[X])

If our system's payoff function f is convex (antifragile), MORE variance in the input X strictly improves the expected payoff. The mathematical heart of antifragility. Mapping. A VOB level that benefits from variance (mixed volume probes) more than constant-magnitude probes is antifragile.

6.3 Work hardening — Hadfield steel and the Hollomon equation

The canonical work-hardening law (Hollomon 1945):

σ = K × εpn

where K is the strength coefficient, εp the plastic strain, n the strain hardening exponent. For most metals n ≈ 0.1–0.3. For Hadfield steel (austenitic Mn steel) n ≈ 0.5 — the highest of any common alloy. Mechanism: stress-induced martensitic transformation locally where stress is highest. The harder the impact, the harder the localized response.

6.3.1 Voce hardening law

σ = σs − (σs − σy) × exp(−ε / ε0)

Better fit for materials with saturating hardening — i.e., materials that work-harden up to a ceiling. Mapping. A VOB level can only work-harden so far (finite pool capacity). Voce gives the saturation curve. Variable: A-VAR-3 sigma_s_estimate.

6.3.2 Ludwik (1909) — strain-hardening with elastic offset

σ = σy + K × εpn

6.3.3 Swift (1952) — strain-hardening with pre-strain offset

σ = K × (ε0 + εp)n

6.3.4 Krupkowski — combined hardening

σ = K × (ε0 + εp)n + σ0

The four equations above bracket all common work-hardening behaviors. Fit each to a zone's touch-history (touch volume vs cumulative strain) and pick the best by AIC. The fitted exponent n is the antifragility-of-hardening variable: A-VAR-4 n_hollomon.

6.4 Wolff's law and Frost's mechanostat — bone remodeling

Wolff (1892): bone density adapts to mechanical stress. Frost (1987) quantified the mechanostat: bone remodels to maintain strain in a target window.

dρbone/dt = k × (σ − σthreshold) when σ > σthreshold (bone DEPOSITION) dρbone/dt = −k' × (σmin − σ) when σ < σmin (bone RESORPTION; disuse atrophy)

Carter-Hayes (1977) density-stiffness relationship:

Ebone = 3790 × ρbone3 (MPa)

Stiffness scales as the CUBE of density — Wolff's amplification. Mapping. A VOB level's stiffness E_VOB scales with the cube of its accumulated pool volume per unit width (analogue of bone density). Variable: A-VAR-5 wolff_stiffness.

6.5 Hormesis — the J-curve dose response

Calabrese-Baldwin (2003) review of hormesis in toxicology: low-dose exposure to a stressor produces an ADAPTIVE response that increases system robustness. Quantitative model:

Response(D) = a + b × log(D) + c × (log(D))2

with parameters fit such that the curve is inverted-U or J-shaped — peak performance at a non-zero intermediate dose. Mapping. Some volume probe magnitude is OPTIMAL for strengthening a VOB level — too small and no response, too large and the level shatters. Variable: A-VAR-6 hormesis_optimum.

6.6 Self-healing materials — restoration after damage

Healing efficiency:

ηheal = (Phealed − Pdamaged) / (Pvirgin − Pdamaged)

For self-healing concrete (HEALCON), polymer microcapsule systems (White et al. 2001), and vascular networks. Mapping. A VOB level can recover after a partial penetration if defenders pile back in. Tracked as the volume returned to the pool within N bars of a penetration event. Variable: A-VAR-7 heal_efficiency.

6.7 Mithridatization kinetics

dCtolerance/dt = kacq × Cexposure − kloss × Ctolerance

Tolerance accumulates with exposure and decays without. Steady-state: Ctolss = (kacq/kloss) × Cexp. Mapping. A VOB level builds tolerance to probes of a given magnitude — once it has weathered enough of them, it has "immunity" to that probe class.

9. Updated I/T/A equations using the v2 variable space

9.1 Imperviousness I(t) — updated

Combining the 15 imperviousness variables via a multiplicative-then-logistic formulation that captures the Tate-Alekseevskii target resistance, Walker-Anderson obliquity, Beer-Lambert HVL, and cross-source confluence:

Iraw(t) = Rtα1 × tier_agreementα2 × √stack × ATR_thin_factor × cos²(obliquity_slope) ÷ (1 + d_price)2.5 × (1 + cross_indicator_confluence)α3 × (1 + htf_alignment)α4 × (1 + vwap_alignment_bonus) × (1 + adx_strength_bonus) × HVL_volume0.5 × (1 + sector_relative_strength) × (book_depth_imbalance OR 1) × (options_strike_density_factor OR 1)

where book_depth_imbalance and options_strike_density default to 1 (neutral) when the data source isn't connected. The defaults preserve the equation's correctness while new sources are added; the OR-1 pattern means each new data source can only IMPROVE the score, not weaken it.

I(t) = σ(αI × (log(Iraw(t)) − βI))

Logistic on log-scale because the raw equation is multiplicative — log transforms the multiplication into addition, which the logistic squashes into [0, 1].

9.2 Tensile T(t) — updated

Combining the 13 tensile variables — toughness integral, fatigue life, hierarchical confluence, plus TV-built-in elasticity proxies:

Traw(t) = v_pool_normβ1 × success_rateβ2 × (1 + K_discrete)β3 × (N_f_remaining / N_f_initial) × sqrt(K_IC) × (1 + rsi_snap_back_quality) × (1 + cci_extremity_score) × hierarchy_score0.4 × volatility_adaptive_envelope_factor × U_r0.3 × U_t0.2
T(t) = σ(αT × (log(Traw(t)) − βT))

For new zones with k = 0 (no touch history), tensile-history variables (T-VAR-2/3/5/6/7/8/9) bootstrap to per-tier Bayesian priors (median values across the past 90 days of zones in this tier × symbol × session_position bucket). This keeps T computable from formation.

9.3 Antifragility A(t) — updated

Araw(t) = (1 + H_taleb)γ1 × max(0.01, S_convexity_c + 1)γ2 × hadfield_ratioγ3 × lindy_term0.5 × heal_efficiencyγ4 × (1 + earnings_survival_bonus) × max(0.5, sigma_s_estimate / pool_current) × (1 + |n_hollomon − 0|) × wolff_stiffness0.2 × (1 + hormesis_optimum_match)
A(t) = σ(αA × (log(Araw(t)) − βA))

Notes: max(0.01, S_convexity_c + 1) keeps A computable even when fitted convexity is slightly negative (small fragility doesn't entirely nullify other antifragility evidence — they sum logically). Earnings-survival is a 1.5× multiplier when the zone has survived its most recent earnings event.

9.4 Master probability P(t) — updated

P(t) = (I(t)wI × T(t)wT × A(t)wA)1 / (wI + wT + wA)

Initial weights: w_I = w_T = w_A = 1 (equal geometric mean). After 90 days, recalibrate via logistic regression with interaction terms:

Pv3(t) = σ(β0 + βI I + βT T + βA A + βIT IT + βIA IA + βTA TA + βITA ITA)

The triple-interaction term β_ITA captures the "all three present" reinforcement that the geometric mean already approximates — the regression measures the deviation from pure geometric-mean behavior.

14. Validation protocol — updated

  1. V1 — Variable correlation audit. 38 variables × outcome (held / broke). Expect ~15 to fail the 0.05 cutoff. Final set ~23.
  2. V2 — Variable decorrelation. PCA on the surviving variables; consolidate redundant ones via factor loading.
  3. V3 — Equation form selection. Test geometric mean, multiplicative, max-of-three, logistic regression, gradient-boosted decision tree on held-out data. Pick by AUROC + calibration.
  4. V4 — Threshold calibration. Isotonic regression on the chosen equation; pick thresholds where realized rate = stated rate.
  5. V5 — Regime partitioning. Re-fit per regime (trend, range, gap-up, gap-down, inside-day, earnings-window).
  6. V6 — Latency profiling. Live measurement: end-to-end < 1 second.
  7. V7 — Fragility validation. F₃ events predict blow-through within 5 bars at > 70% rate.
  8. V8 — A/B canary. 14 days shadow mode vs canonical VOB. Promote when all metrics win.

18. Glossary

TermMeaning
R_t (Tate target resistance)Per Tate (1967): the effective resistance a target offers to a long-rod projectile. Typically 4–6× static yield strength. In TIA: pool × tier_agreement × stack_count.
Half-Value Layer (HVL)Per Beer-Lambert (§4.4.1): the thickness of shielding required to reduce radiation intensity to half. In TIA: volume movement to halve defending pool.
Salame permachorAggregated polymer cohesion parameter used to predict permeability. In TIA: aggregated cohesion across supporting indicators.
Hollomon exponent nStrain-hardening exponent. Most metals 0.1–0.3; Hadfield steel ≈ 0.5. In TIA: fitted from log-log pool-vs-strain.
Taleb-Douady HThe discrete-second-difference antifragility detector: (f(X+K)+f(X−K))/2 − f(X). Positive = antifragile.
Wolff stiffnessCarter-Hayes E = 3790 × ρ³. In TIA: zone stiffness scales as the cube of pool density.
Hormesis optimumThe non-zero dose at which a stressor produces maximum adaptive benefit. In TIA: the optimal touch-volume magnitude for level-strengthening.
Hadfield ratioE[v|rejected] / E[v|penetrated]. > 1 = high-vol tests held; antifragile.
